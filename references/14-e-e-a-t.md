**E-E-A-T: Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness**

**Why This File Exists**

E-E-A-T is the framework Google uses - through its human Search Quality Raters and its algorithmic quality systems - to evaluate whether a site deserves to rank. It is not a direct ranking factor in the way page speed or backlinks are, but it shapes nearly every signal Google uses. Sites that demonstrate E-E-A-T win. Sites that don't get suppressed, especially after core updates.

Most site owners hear "E-E-A-T" and assume it's only relevant for medical or financial sites. That's wrong. E-E-A-T applies to every site, on every topic. The bar is just higher for topics where bad information causes real harm.

This file gives you the framework, the specific signals Google uses to evaluate it, and the systematic process to build it on any site.

**What E-E-A-T Actually Stands For**

Originally E-A-T (Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness). Google added the second E - Experience - in late 2022 specifically to recognize that first-hand experience often matters more than formal credentials.

**Experience** - Has the author actually done, used, lived, visited, or directly observed the thing they're writing about?

**Expertise** - Does the author have the knowledge, skills, or training to speak credibly on the topic?

**Authoritativeness** - Is the author or site recognized by others in the field as a go-to source on this topic?

**Trustworthiness** - Is the site honest, accurate, transparent, and safe? Trust is the most important of the four - without it, the others don't matter.

Google's documentation explicitly states trust is the foundation. Experience, expertise, and authoritativeness all serve to establish trust. A site can be authoritative and untrustworthy (think of a famous site that publishes inaccurate content). It cannot rank long-term without trust.

**YMYL: When E-E-A-T Matters Most**

YMYL stands for "Your Money or Your Life." These are topics where inaccurate or deceptive content can directly harm someone's:

- Health (medical, mental health, drugs, treatments, diagnoses)
- Financial wellbeing (investing, taxes, loans, retirement, major purchases)
- Safety (legal advice, civic information, emergency response)
- Major life decisions (parenting, relationships, education, career)
- Groups of people (news, civic information, content about communities)

For YMYL topics, Google holds E-E-A-T to its highest standard. Sites without demonstrated expertise in YMYL niches will not rank, no matter how well-optimized the content is technically. This is by design and will not change.

For non-YMYL topics, E-E-A-T still matters but the threshold is lower. A hobby site about model trains doesn't need credentialed experts - it needs people with genuine experience and knowledge of model trains. That's still E-E-A-T; it's just calibrated to the topic.

**The Four Components in Practice**

**Experience**

Signals that demonstrate first-hand experience:

- Original photos taken by the author (not stock images)
- Original screenshots showing the author actually used a product or tool
- Specific stories with verifiable detail ("In 2019 I worked with…", "On my third visit to…")
- Process documentation that could only come from doing the thing
- Honest reporting of what didn't work, not just what did
- Content that includes failures, mistakes, surprises - markers of real engagement

For your situation as a Christian author and library operator: experience signals include your years of writing, the specific spiritual journey you can describe, the testimonies you've witnessed, the books you've personally read and applied, the conversations with real people that shaped your perspective. None of this can be faked, and all of it can be made visible on the site.

**Expertise**

Signals that demonstrate expertise:

- Visible credentials (degrees, certifications, ordinations, professional memberships)
- Years of practice or work in the field
- Published works (books, peer-reviewed articles, speaking engagements)
- Quality of explanation - experts simplify without dumbing down
- Accuracy of technical detail, terminology, and nuance
- Awareness of common misconceptions and ability to correct them

Expertise can be formal (degrees, licenses) or earned (a self-taught practitioner with 20 years of work in a craft). Google recognizes both, but earned expertise needs more demonstration to be credible.

**Authoritativeness**

Signals that demonstrate authority:

- Backlinks from other recognized sites in the field
- Mentions in trade publications, news outlets, podcasts, conferences
- Citations of the author's work by others
- Original research, frameworks, or terminology that others adopt
- Consistent topical coverage over years, not weeks
- Industry recognition (awards, panels, board roles)

Authority is largely external - it's what others say about you, not what you say about yourself. This is why claiming authority on your own site without external validation rings hollow.

**Trustworthiness**

Signals that demonstrate trust:

- Real author identity, not anonymous or pseudonymous
- Working contact information (not just a form)
- Physical address if applicable
- Privacy policy and terms of service
- HTTPS and secure technical setup
- Accurate information with clear sourcing
- Visible correction policy (and corrections when made)
- Transparent disclosure of affiliate relationships, sponsorships, AI assistance
- No deceptive practices (cloaked content, hidden text, misleading headlines)
- Reasonable, accurate, fulfilled promises (especially for transactional pages)

Trust is binary in many ways. A single discovered deception can destroy years of accumulated trust. This is why short-term tactics that compromise trust - fake reviews, undisclosed paid links, AI content presented as human, exaggerated claims - backfire even when they appear to work in the moment.

**Author Pages: The Foundation**

Every piece of content with a named author should link to a dedicated author page. This is one of the highest-leverage E-E-A-T improvements most sites are missing.

**What Belongs on an Author Page**

- **Real name** (not a pen name, unless the pen name is publicly known and consistently used)
- **Photo** - a clear professional headshot, not a cartoon avatar or stock image
- **Bio** - 200-400 words covering background, qualifications, and topical focus
- **Credentials** - degrees, certifications, training, ordination, professional memberships
- **Years of experience** in the relevant field
- **Notable accomplishments** - books published, organizations founded, speaking history, awards
- **Links to other published work** - books, articles, podcasts, interviews, guest posts
- **Links to professional profiles** - LinkedIn, Goodreads, Amazon author page, denominational directories, industry associations
- **Topics they cover** on this site
- **Contact information** or a way to reach them
- **List of all articles** they've written on the site
- **Schema markup** identifying the author (Person schema with sameAs links to verified profiles)

**Bio Writing Principles**

Author bios should be specific, not generic. Compare:

Generic: "John is a passionate writer who loves helping people achieve their best life."

Specific: "John has spent 15 years pastoring small congregations across rural Tennessee. He's the author of three books on local church revitalization and has trained over 200 lay leaders through the Heartland Pastors Network."

The second version is short, but every sentence carries verifiable specifics. Specifics build trust. Generic claims destroy it.

**Multi-Author Sites**

If multiple people contribute to a site, each needs their own author page with the full treatment above. A common failure mode is having one strong founder bio and then attributing dozens of articles to "Staff" or unnamed contributors. This signals low E-E-A-T and Google penalizes it.

If you genuinely have ghostwritten or AI-assisted content, name the editor or sponsor of the content who takes responsibility for accuracy.

**About Page: The Site's Story**

Beyond individual author pages, the site needs an About page that establishes the site's authority and trustworthiness as a whole.

**What Belongs on an About Page**

- **The site's purpose** - why it exists, what problem it solves, who it serves
- **Origin story** - when it started, who started it, why
- **Editorial standards** - how content is created, fact-checked, reviewed, and updated
- **Team and contributors** - who's behind the site (with links to their author pages)
- **Track record** - accomplishments, milestones, reach (if meaningful), notable mentions
- **Mission and values** - especially important for content sites with a worldview or philosophy
- **Affiliations and disclosures** - partnerships, sponsorships, parent organizations
- **Contact information** - multiple ways to reach you
- **Physical location** if applicable

About pages that read like marketing copy don't build trust. About pages that read like an honest account of who you are and why you do this work do.

**Contact and Trust Pages**

Google's Quality Rater Guidelines explicitly call out the importance of clear contact information, especially for YMYL sites. Required pages or sections:

- **Contact page** with email address (working), physical mailing address (if applicable), phone (if applicable), and a contact form as a backup
- **Privacy policy** - what data you collect, how you use it, third-party sharing, user rights
- **Terms of service** - for any site that takes payments, has user accounts, or hosts user-generated content
- **Cookie policy** - required for sites with EU traffic
- **Editorial policy** - how content is sourced, reviewed, updated; especially important for news, health, finance
- **Corrections policy** - how errors are handled when found
- **Disclosure policy** - affiliate links, sponsored content, AI assistance, free product reviews

Sites that hide contact information or operate with no clear ownership are heavily suppressed by Google's quality systems. Anonymity in 2026 is treated as a near-automatic trust failure for any site that's monetized or making substantive claims.

**Citations and Sourcing**

Content that makes substantive claims needs sources. The standards:

- **Cite primary sources** when possible (original studies, official documents, named experts) over secondary sources (other articles citing the original)
- **Link to sources directly** so readers can verify
- **Use authoritative sources** - government data, peer-reviewed research, recognized institutions, named experts in the field
- **Avoid circular sourcing** - don't cite another article that's citing the same thing you are
- **Date your claims** - "as of January 2026" makes content honest about when it was current
- **Update content with new sources** when information changes

For Christian content specifically: cite scripture clearly with version (NIV, ESV, KJV), book, chapter, verse. Cite specific teachings or sermons with attribution. Distinguish between scripture, commentary, tradition, and personal interpretation. This level of clarity demonstrates expertise readers and Google both recognize.

**Original Research and Frameworks**

The strongest E-E-A-T signal is producing content others cite. This means:

- **Original research** - surveys, data analysis, experiments, case studies
- **Original frameworks** - named methodologies, models, processes that organize a topic in a new way
- **Original definitions or terminology** - naming a phenomenon others were observing but not articulating
- **Original synthesis** - combining sources or angles in a way that becomes the standard reference

These are hard to produce, which is exactly why they're high-value. A single piece of original research, well-promoted, can earn more high-quality backlinks than 100 derivative articles.

For your situation, original research could include: surveys of Christian readers about reading habits, original analysis of how Zig Ziglar's principles map to specific scriptures, case studies of churches applying Mutual Success Team principles, your own framework for spiritual formation through reading.

**Topical Authority: The Long Game**

Google increasingly evaluates sites holistically. Are you covering a topic in depth, or scratching the surface across many topics? Topical authority is built systematically:

**The Cluster Model**

Pick core topics where you have genuine expertise. For each core topic:

- One pillar page covering the topic comprehensively
- 5-15 cluster posts diving deep into specific aspects
- Internal links connecting cluster posts to the pillar and to each other
- Author bios on each that demonstrate expertise on this specific topic
- External links earned over time from related sites

A site with three deeply-developed topical clusters outranks a site with 30 shallow ones, even if the shallow site has more total content.

**Depth Over Breadth**

When deciding whether to publish on a new topic, ask:

- Do we have genuine expertise here, or are we just chasing keywords?
- Can we go deeper than what already ranks?
- Will publishing here strengthen or dilute our topical focus?

Most sites would benefit from publishing less but deeper. The temptation to cover everything is what produces sites Google can't categorize and therefore can't rank.

**Time on Topic**

Topical authority compounds over time. A site that's been writing about a specific subject for five years has signals a six-month-old site cannot replicate, regardless of content quality. This is one reason new sites struggle in established niches and why patience plus consistency wins long-term.

**E-E-A-T for Specific Site Types**

**For Author or Creator Sites**

Your strongest signal is the body of work itself. Books published, courses created, articles written, speaking done. The site should foreground this work prominently. Author pages should function as portfolios. Original content (not just reviews of others' work) should dominate the site.

**For Library or Curation Sites**

Sites that compile or curate content from others (like TeamSuccessNetwork) face a specific E-E-A-T challenge: the value isn't just the content, it's the curation. To demonstrate this:

- Editorial standards page explaining how books are selected
- Curator bios with qualifications to evaluate the field
- Original commentary on each curated piece (why it's included, who it serves)
- Original organizational frameworks (how the library is structured)
- Original synthesis content (reading guides, comparative analyses, themed collections)
- Author pages for the curators themselves

A library that's just a list of books with no curator presence is harder to rank than one where the curators visibly shape and explain the collection.

**For Ecommerce Sites**

Trust signals dominate. Product pages need real photos, accurate descriptions, honest reviews (with verification), clear return policies, secure checkout, and visible business identity. Brand sites that demonstrate the people behind the product (founders, team, manufacturing, sourcing) outperform faceless sites.

**For Local Business Sites**

Local trust signals: real address (verified via Google Business Profile), real photos of the location and team, real customer reviews, local affiliations and licensing, years in business. The closer the business is to the customer, the more these signals matter.

**For Affiliate or Review Sites**

These are the hardest E-E-A-T sites to rank. Required: actual hands-on experience with products reviewed (not just spec sheets), original photos and testing, clear disclosure of affiliate relationships, balanced reviews (not just promotion), reviewer credentials relevant to the product category. Sites that review products without ever having used them are explicitly targeted by Google's quality systems.

**Schema Markup for E-E-A-T**

Structured data helps Google understand who's behind your content. Key schemas:

- **Person schema** for each author page, with sameAs links to LinkedIn, professional profiles, and verified accounts
- **Article schema** with author property linked to the Person schema
- **Organization schema** for the site itself
- **AboutPage schema** for the About page
- **ContactPage schema** for the Contact page
- **Review schema** for review content (with proper reviewer attribution)
- **Course schema** if you publish educational content

These don't replace the actual content but help search engines reliably extract who, what, and how authoritative.

For implementation details, see references/schema-markup.md.

**Common E-E-A-T Mistakes**

**Anonymous content** - articles with no author attribution. Fix: real bylines on everything.

**Stock photo "team" pages** - fake team photos signal fakery. Fix: real photos or no team page.

**Generic author bios** - "John loves writing about lifestyle and wellness." Fix: specific, verifiable bios.

**Credentials buried or missing** - qualifications not visible on author pages. Fix: prominent display.

**No external validation** - site claims authority that nothing outside the site supports. Fix: build genuine external presence.

**Outdated content presented as current** - articles from 2018 with no update notes still ranking for "current best practices." Fix: dated content with update history, or removal.

**Inconsistent identity** - different names, photos, or bios for the same person across the web. Fix: consistent identity everywhere.

**Hidden ownership** - site won't disclose who runs it or where they're located. Fix: transparency.

**AI content without human accountability** - pages with no named author behind AI-assisted content. Fix: named editors who take responsibility.

**Reviews without reviewer identity** - five-star reviews from "Anonymous." Fix: verified reviewer programs, real names where possible.

**Building E-E-A-T Systematically**

A 90-day plan to substantially improve E-E-A-T on any site:

**Days 1-30: Foundation**

- Create or rewrite author pages for every contributor (real photo, specific bio, credentials, contact)
- Rewrite About page with origin story, editorial standards, team, mission
- Add or update Contact page with multiple working contact methods
- Add Privacy Policy, Terms of Service, Cookie Policy, Editorial Policy, Corrections Policy as applicable
- Implement Person schema for authors and Organization schema for the site
- Audit existing content for unattributed pieces and add real bylines

**Days 31-60: Content Signals**

- Audit top 20 pages for E-E-A-T signals; add experience markers, citations, and original perspective where missing
- Add author bio boxes at the bottom of every article linking to author pages
- Replace stock photos with original images where feasible
- Add publication and update dates to all articles
- Implement Article schema with author attribution

**Days 61-90: External Authority**

- Identify 10 industry publications, podcasts, or sites where guest contribution would build authority
- Pursue 3-5 guest opportunities to establish external presence
- Submit author profiles to relevant directories (HARO/Qwoted for sourcing, industry associations, denominational directories)
- Encourage and respond to reviews on third-party platforms (Google Business, industry-specific review sites)
- Begin original research project (survey, analysis, framework) for publication in months 4-6

After 90 days, the site has demonstrably stronger E-E-A-T signals across foundation, content, and external dimensions. Compounding begins.

**The Test**

Ask of any page on your site: if a reader landed here from Google search results, would they:

- Know who wrote this and trust their qualifications?
- Believe the information is accurate and current?
- Find the site overall to be honest, transparent, and safe?
- See evidence the author has actual experience with the topic?
- Want to read more from this author or site?

If the answer to any of these is no, that's the E-E-A-T gap. Close it.

**Final Principle**

E-E-A-T is not a checklist of metadata. It's the cumulative effect of operating a site like a real, accountable, expert-led publication over time. Sites that try to fake it produce surface-level signals that Google's systems increasingly see through. Sites that earn it produce signals that hold through every algorithm update.

The good news: most websites do not take E-E-A-T seriously. Genuinely investing in it is one of the largest competitive moats available, especially in crowded niches.

The harder news: there is no shortcut. Real expertise, real experience, real authority, and real trust take years to build and seconds to lose. Build accordingly.
