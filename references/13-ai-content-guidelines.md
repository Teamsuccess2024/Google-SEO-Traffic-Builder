**AI Content Guidelines for SEO**

**Why This File Exists**

AI content tools have changed publishing forever. Used well, they let small teams produce work that rivals teams ten times their size. Used poorly, they generate the exact kind of low-value content Google built its 2022-2024 algorithm updates to destroy.

This file exists because most "AI content for SEO" advice is one of two extremes: either "AI is fine, just publish it" or "Google will penalize anything AI touches." Both are wrong. The truth requires more nuance, and that nuance is the difference between scaling a content operation and watching it collapse.

This file gives you the operating principles that keep AI-assisted content profitable, durable, and rankable.

**The One-Sentence Rule**

Google does not penalize AI content. Google penalizes unhelpful content. Some AI content is helpful. Most published AI content is not. Your job is to be in the first group.

Everything in this file is downstream of that sentence.

**What Google Actually Says**

Google's official position, stated repeatedly since 2023:

- AI-generated content is allowed
- Content created primarily to manipulate rankings is not allowed, regardless of whether AI or humans wrote it
- The same quality standards apply to AI and human content: helpful, reliable, people-first, demonstrating experience and expertise
- Automation used to scale content without quality control is treated as spam

Translation: Google doesn't care how content is made. It cares whether the content deserves to rank. AI doesn't change that calculation - it just makes it easier to produce content that doesn't deserve to rank, faster.

**The Helpful Content System (HCS)**

Google rolled the Helpful Content Update into its core ranking system in 2024. It's not a separate update anymore - it's part of how Google evaluates every page, every day.

The HCS asks questions like:

- Was this written for humans or for search engines?
- Does it demonstrate first-hand experience with the topic?
- Does the author have evident expertise?
- Does it leave the reader feeling they got useful information?
- Does it provide substantial value compared to other results for the query?
- Is it the kind of page someone would bookmark, share, or recommend?

Sites that score poorly on these questions get site-wide demotions, not just per-page demotions. One bad section of your site can drag down everything else. This is why AI content strategy must be a site-wide discipline, not a per-post decision.

**When AI Is Fine**

AI assistance is appropriate and effective in these scenarios:

**Research synthesis** - Pulling together information from sources you've gathered, organizing it into a coherent structure. AI is excellent at this. The expertise is yours; the formatting labor is offloaded.

**Outline generation** - Producing a strong skeleton based on a clear prompt with target keyword, audience, and intent. You then fill it with your knowledge.

**First drafts of structured content** - Comparison tables, glossary entries, FAQ sections, definitional content where the answer is largely known and the value is in clear presentation.

**Editing and rewriting** - Tightening prose, fixing grammar, varying sentence structure, adapting tone. AI is a strong editor when given clear direction.

**Translation and localization** - Adapting content for different markets, with native review.

**Schema markup generation** - Producing JSON-LD code from existing content. Mechanical work AI handles cleanly.

**Meta titles and descriptions at scale** - Writing 100 product page metas where each follows a clear formula. Repetitive, formula-driven, perfect for AI.

**Internal linking suggestions** - Identifying contextual link opportunities across a content library.

**Content briefs** - Producing detailed briefs that human writers then fulfill.

**Repurposing across formats** - Turning a long article into a social thread, an email, a video script. Same source material, different containers.

In all of these cases, AI is augmenting work that has a clear human source of truth and judgment behind it.

**When AI Kills Rankings**

AI use becomes dangerous in these scenarios:

**Topic generation without expertise** - Asking AI "what should I write about for \[niche\]?" and publishing what it suggests. The output will be generic, derivative, and indistinguishable from every other AI-generated site in the niche.

**Full-article generation with no human input** - Prompting "write a 2,000-word article about X" and publishing the result. This produces the median of the existing internet on that topic, which by definition doesn't deserve to rank.

**Programmatic content at scale** - Generating thousands of pages from templates and AI fill (e.g., "best \[product\] in \[city\]" pages for every city). Google's site reputation systems specifically target this pattern.

**YMYL content without credentials** - Your Money or Your Life topics (health, finance, legal, safety) require demonstrated expertise. AI cannot provide that. Publishing AI-written medical, financial, or legal content without expert review is a fast track to penalties.

**News and current events** - AI content has stale knowledge and no access to real-time facts. Using it for news topics produces inaccurate content that ranks briefly, then gets buried.

**Personal narrative or experience content** - "My experience with X" written by AI is fabrication. Reviews, case studies, personal essays, anything claiming first-hand experience cannot be AI-generated without crossing into deceptive territory.

**Content where the AI hallucinated facts you didn't verify** - AI confidently produces wrong information. Publishing unverified AI claims about statistics, quotes, dates, citations, or technical details is how sites lose trust with both Google and readers.

The pattern across all of these: AI is being asked to provide expertise, judgment, or experience it doesn't have. When that happens, the content fails the helpfulness test.

**The Human-in-the-Loop Requirement**

Every piece of AI-assisted content that gets published needs a human providing at least one of the following:

- **Original expertise** - knowledge the AI doesn't have because it's specific to the author's experience, business, research, or field
- **Original perspective** - a point of view, framework, or argument the author brings that AI is following, not generating
- **Original verification** - fact-checking, source-verifying, and confirming accuracy of every substantive claim
- **Original synthesis** - combining sources or angles in a way that reflects human judgment about what matters

If a piece of content offers none of these, it shouldn't be published - not because of Google, but because there's no reason for it to exist.

The minimum acceptable workflow:

- Human defines the topic, angle, target audience, and unique value proposition
- AI assists with research, structure, or drafting
- Human edits substantively - adding examples, removing generic filler, inserting specific knowledge
- Human verifies every factual claim
- Human reads the final piece as a reader would and asks: "Did I get value from this?"

If step 5's answer is "not really," the piece isn't ready. AI made it efficient to produce; human judgment makes it worth publishing.

**The Editing Test**

After AI produces a draft, the human edit must accomplish at least three of the following:

- Add a specific example, story, or case from the author's experience
- Remove generic statements that could appear on any site in the niche
- Correct or sharpen claims that were vague, hedged, or unclear
- Insert original opinions, frameworks, or recommendations
- Add internal links that reflect understanding of the site's broader content
- Restructure for the actual reader's journey, not the AI's default outline
- Cut length aggressively - most AI drafts are 30-50% padding

A piece that comes out of AI and gets published with light copy-editing has not been edited. It's been proofread. Proofreading is not the standard.

**Detecting AI Content (And Why It Matters)**

Google likely uses AI detection signals, but reliable AI detection is technically very hard, and Google has not stated it uses detection as a ranking factor. What Google can detect, and uses heavily:

- Patterns of low engagement (high bounce, short time on page)
- Lack of unique value compared to competing pages
- Stylistic uniformity across many pages on a site
- Sudden volume increases without traffic growth
- Thin or templated structure
- Absence of E-E-A-T signals
- Low return-visitor rates

These signals correlate strongly with AI-generated content but are about quality, not provenance. A site producing low-quality human content fails the same tests. The lesson: optimize for actual reader value, and detection becomes irrelevant.

**Disclosure**

Whether to disclose AI use is partly an ethical question and partly a strategic one.

- **Required** in some jurisdictions and industries (regulated content, journalism in some markets)
- **Reader-trust positive** in technical, educational, and creator audiences
- **Reader-trust neutral or negative** in some commercial contexts where readers expect human work

Google's position: disclosure is encouraged but not mandatory for ranking. They don't reward or punish based on disclosure alone. They reward quality.

Recommendation: when AI played a substantial role in producing content, a simple author note ("AI-assisted research, written and edited by \[Name\]") is honest, takes nothing from the work, and builds long-term trust.

**Voice and Style Considerations**

AI-written content has tells. Even strong AI output trends toward:

- Hedge phrases ("it's important to note," "in the world of," "when it comes to")
- Listicle structure with predictable subheadings
- Balanced both-sides framing on topics with clear answers
- Generic transitions ("furthermore," "additionally," "moreover")
- Polished neutrality that reads like nobody actually wrote it
- Padding sentences that restate the previous sentence

Your edit must remove these. Specifically:

- Cut hedge phrases unless genuine uncertainty exists
- Vary section structure - don't let every section follow the same pattern
- Take a position when you have one
- Use direct transitions or none at all
- Inject voice - opinions, asides, specific word choices that reflect the author
- Cut every sentence that doesn't add information

Brand voice consistency is impossible to maintain at AI scale without a documented voice guide and active enforcement. Sites that scale AI content without a voice discipline produce a flat, interchangeable feel that readers and Google both notice.

**E-E-A-T and AI**

E-E-A-T (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) is Google's quality framework. AI-assisted content has to address each component deliberately:

**Experience** - AI has none. The author must supply it. Personal stories, case examples, "I tried this and here's what happened" - these are non-negotiable for content where experience is relevant.

**Expertise** - AI can synthesize public expertise but doesn't have its own. The author's qualifications, credentials, or demonstrated track record must be visible somewhere on the site (author bio, about page, schema markup with author info).

**Authoritativeness** - Built through external signals (backlinks, mentions, citations) and internal signals (consistent topical coverage, depth). AI can produce volume; only humans can produce the kind of work that earns links.

**Trustworthiness** - Accuracy, transparency, citation of sources, working contact information, real author identity. AI-assisted content that fakes any of these crosses into deceptive territory and is treated accordingly.

For a deeper treatment, read references/e-e-a-t.md.

**Programmatic SEO and AI**

Programmatic SEO - generating many pages from templates plus structured data - predates AI. AI made it faster but didn't change the rules.

Acceptable programmatic content:

- Each page provides genuinely unique, useful information
- Underlying data is accurate, current, and verified
- Pages serve real search demand from real users
- Templates produce variation that reflects the actual differences in source data
- Site has clear value proposition beyond the template fill

Unacceptable programmatic content:

- Pages generated for every possible keyword variation regardless of search demand
- Templated content with only minor data substitutions across thousands of pages
- AI-filled descriptions to bulk up otherwise thin pages
- "Best X in Y city" pages where the data is invented or low-quality

The site reputation abuse policy (rolled out in 2024) specifically targets large-scale AI/programmatic content on otherwise reputable sites. A trusted domain hosting low-quality AI content gets the low-quality content de-indexed even if the rest of the site is fine.

**Workflow: Producing AI-Assisted Content That Ranks**

A repeatable workflow for content that uses AI without becoming AI slop:

**Step 1 - Define Before You Generate**

Before any AI prompt, document:

- Target keyword and search intent
- Specific reader and what they need
- The unique angle or value this piece provides versus existing top results
- The author's relevant experience or expertise
- The desired tone and voice

If you can't fill these in, AI cannot rescue the piece. Don't proceed.

**Step 2 - Research First, Generate Second**

Gather sources before drafting. Either:

- Have AI summarize sources you provide (lower hallucination risk)
- Or do research yourself and feed AI the findings to organize

Never let AI invent sources or statistics without verification.

**Step 3 - Draft with Constraints**

Prompt with the brief from Step 1 included. Specify:

- Exact word count target
- Required sections
- Required examples or data points
- Tone and audience
- What to avoid (clichés, hedge phrases, generic openings)

Strong prompts produce drafts that need less rebuilding.

**Step 4 - Edit Substantively**

Apply the editing test (above). Add what AI couldn't:

- Specific stories
- Original opinions
- Concrete examples from your work
- Internal links to other pages on your site
- Sharper, less-hedged claims

**Step 5 - Verify Every Claim**

Fact-check:

- All statistics
- All quotes
- All historical dates
- All technical specifications
- All source attributions

If a claim can't be verified, cut it or replace it with something that can.

**Step 6 - Add Human Signals**

Ensure the final piece has:

- A real author name and bio
- Original images or screenshots where relevant (not stock or AI-generated)
- Internal links showing site context
- A clear CTA tied to the user's actual journey

**Step 7 - Publish, Measure, Iterate**

After publishing, track via references/analytics-tracking.md. Pages that don't engage readers within 30-60 days don't get better with time. Either substantially improve them or remove them.

**Volume Discipline**

The temptation with AI is to publish more. The discipline is to publish what's worth publishing.

Healthy patterns:

- 2-4 high-quality posts per week beats 20 mediocre posts per week
- Updating and improving existing content beats adding new thin content
- Building topical depth in fewer areas beats shallow coverage across many

Unhealthy patterns:

- Sudden 10x increases in publishing volume after adopting AI
- Targeting every adjacent keyword regardless of audience overlap
- Publishing because the AI made it easy, not because the content was needed

Google's algorithms are calibrated to detect site-wide quality dilution. A few great pages on a site full of AI filler still get suppressed because the site signals as low-quality overall.

**Recovering From AI Content That Hurt You**

If a site has been damaged by published AI content (traffic dropped after a core update, or pages stopped indexing):

- **Audit honestly** - review every published AI-assisted page against the helpfulness criteria. Be ruthless.
- **Categorize each page:**
  - **Keep and improve** - has potential, needs substantial human additions
  - **Consolidate** - merge with a stronger page on the same topic
  - **Remove** - delete and apply 410 status (gone permanently) or redirect to relevant content if applicable
- **Improve at scale** - for keepers, add the elements that were missing: experience, examples, original perspective, verified facts.
- **Trim aggressively** - for sites with hundreds of weak pages, removing them often improves overall site signals more than adding new content.
- **Wait for re-evaluation** - Google's quality systems re-evaluate sites continuously, but recovery typically takes 3-9 months even after fixes are in place. Don't expect immediate rebound.
- **Document the change** - keep notes on what was changed and when. If a future core update brings recovery, you'll know what worked.

**A Realistic Example**

A workable AI-assisted production cycle for a single 1,500-word article:

- 30 minutes - define the brief, identify the angle, gather sources
- 15 minutes - AI generates first draft from the brief
- 60 minutes - substantive human edit: add examples, cut padding, sharpen claims, insert internal links
- 20 minutes - fact-check every substantive claim
- 15 minutes - final read, formatting, schema, publish

Total: roughly 2.5 hours of human time per piece. For comparison, writing the same piece from scratch might take 4-6 hours. AI saves time. It does not eliminate the work that makes content rank.

If your AI workflow takes 20 minutes per piece, you're producing the kind of content this file was written to warn against.

**What "Working" Looks Like With AI Content**

Sites using AI well show:

- Steady traffic growth, not spikes followed by drops
- Engagement metrics matching or exceeding human-only content
- Backlinks earned organically (other sites cite your work)
- Pages ranking for queries beyond their target keyword (sign of real topical authority)
- Brand search growth (people searching for the site by name)
- Email subscribers, return visitors, conversions following the same trends as traffic

Sites using AI poorly show:

- Volume up, traffic flat or declining
- High bounce, low time on page
- Pages stuck on page 3+ no matter how long they're live
- No backlinks because the content isn't worth citing
- Brand search flat (no one is forming an opinion of the site)
- Drops with each core update

The numbers tell the truth. If a content strategy is working, it shows up in the data within 4-6 months. If it's not, AI volume won't fix it - it will accelerate the decline.

**Final Principle**

AI is an amplifier. It amplifies whatever is already there. A site with clear expertise, distinct voice, and disciplined editing standards uses AI to produce more of that. A site without those things uses AI to produce more nothing. Google is increasingly skilled at detecting which is which.

The question to ask before publishing any AI-assisted piece is not "will Google catch this?" The question is "would I be proud to put my name on this?" If yes, publish. If no, the AI made the work easier but not worth doing.

Use AI to multiply your real value. Never to substitute for it.
