**Content Brief Template**

**Why This Template Exists**

A content brief is the bridge between strategy and writing. Without it, even talented writers produce content that ranks accidentally if at all. With it, ordinary writers produce content that ranks consistently because every decision - what to cover, how deeply, in what order, with what links - has been made before drafting begins.

This template gives every piece of content the same disciplined preparation. Writers don't have to guess what's required. SEO isn't bolted on after the fact. The piece is built to rank from the first sentence.

This template is used for every piece of content created under this skill - blog posts, pillar pages, product pages, landing pages, category pages, book pages. The structure adapts; the discipline doesn't.

**How To Use This Template**

Three workflows:

- **Claude fills the brief, user writes** - Claude produces the brief based on keyword research, intent analysis, and SERP review; user or writer drafts from it
- **Claude fills the brief, Claude writes** - full pipeline from research to published draft
- **User fills the brief, Claude writes** - user provides the strategic decisions; Claude executes

Whichever workflow, the brief is completed in full before drafting starts. Skipping fields produces weaker content. The brief takes 20-40 minutes to complete properly. The content takes hours. A weak brief wastes the larger investment.

**The Brief Structure**

The full brief has 18 sections. Some apply to every content type; some are situational (noted inline). Work through them in order - each section's answer informs the next.

**Section 1: Strategic Context**

**Target Keyword (Primary)** The single keyword this piece is built to rank for. One keyword, exact phrasing as searchers type it.

Example: "what does it mean to seek god's kingdom first"

**Secondary Keywords** 3-8 related variations and long-tail phrases the same content can naturally cover. These do not get their own pages - they're absorbed into this one.

Example:

- "seeking the kingdom of god first
- "how to seek god's kingdom first"
- "matthew 6:33 meaning"
- "putting god first in life"

**Topic Cluster** Which content cluster this piece belongs to (from the keyword research sheet).

Example: "Kingdom-Seeking"

**Funnel Stage** TOFU / MOFU / BOFU

**Search Intent** Informational / Navigational / Commercial / Transactional

**Why This Piece Now** One sentence on why this piece is being created and what gap it fills in the existing content library.

Example: "Anchor pillar for the Kingdom-Seeking cluster; current site has no top-of-funnel entry point for readers searching this exact phrase."

**Section 2: Audience and Reader Profile**

**Specific Reader** Describe the exact person searching this query - life situation, what's prompting the search, what they hope to find. Specificity matters; "Christian readers" is not enough.

Example: "Mid-30s to mid-50s Christian, often a believer for several years who has hit a season where work, family, or finances feel like they're crowding out their relationship with God. Searching to understand the practical meaning of Jesus's command, not the academic theology."

**What They Already Know** What context does the reader bring? What can be assumed?

Example: "Familiar with the verse but unsure of its practical application. Knows basic Christian vocabulary. Doesn't need an introduction to who Jesus is."

**What They Don't Know** What gaps does this piece need to fill?

Example: "Doesn't know how to translate 'seek first' into daily decisions. Doesn't know what 'kingdom' specifically means. Confused by the promise that 'all these things will be added.'"

**Their Real Question Behind the Search** What are they actually trying to figure out, beneath the keyword phrasing?

Example: "Am I doing this Christian life wrong? How do I prioritize God when life keeps demanding so much?"

**Emotional State** How does the reader feel arriving at this piece? This shapes tone.

Example: "Tired, mildly guilty, hoping for clarity rather than condemnation. Open but skeptical of pat answers."

**Section 3: SERP Analysis**

Before writing, examine what currently ranks for the primary keyword. This shapes both content direction and competitive positioning.

**Top 5 Currently Ranking URLs** List them.

**SERP Features Present** Note what's appearing in the search results:

- Featured snippet (yes/no, what type)
- People Also Ask box (yes/no, what questions)
- AI Overview (yes/no)
- Video carousel
- Image pack
- Knowledge panel
- Shopping results

**What the Top Results Have in Common** Patterns: format (listicle, guide, sermon transcript), length, structure, depth, types of sources cited.

**What They're Missing** Opportunities: questions not answered, angles not covered, depth not provided, audiences not served.

**How This Piece Will Differ** The specific positioning that will earn the ranking. "Better than competitors" is not enough; identify what specifically will be better and why.

Example: "Top results focus on academic exposition of the Greek for 'kingdom.' This piece will lead with a practical reorientation framework, ground it in the verse, and cite first-hand experience of applying it during specific life pressures. Reader gets clarity plus a method, not just a definition."

**Section 4: Format and Structure**

**Content Type** Choose the closest match:

- Pillar page (comprehensive guide on a broad topic)
- Cluster post (deep dive on one aspect of a pillar topic)
- Listicle ("X best/top/ways to...")
- Comparison ("X vs Y," "X compared to Y")
- How-to / tutorial
- Definition / explainer
- Personal essay or commentary
- Case study
- Review
- Resource roundup
- News or commentary on current event

**Word Count Target** A range, not a single number. Use SERP analysis as a guide:

- If top results average 800 words, target 1,200-1,500
- If top results average 2,500 words, target 2,800-3,500
- For pillar pages, typically 2,500-5,000 words
- For focused cluster posts, typically 1,200-2,000 words

Word count is a target, not a quota. Better content with fewer words beats padded content with more.

**Reading Level** Match the audience. Most general audiences read best at 7th-9th grade level. Technical audiences may want higher; some popular audiences need lower.

**Tone** Specific descriptors. "Conversational" alone is not enough.

Example: "Warm but direct. Speaks to the reader as a respected friend who can handle truth. Uses 'you' throughout. Not preachy. No church-insider jargon without explanation."

**Voice Markers** Specific phrasing patterns, vocabulary choices, or stylistic moves that match the site's voice. For sites with strong author voice, list specifics.

Example: "Em dashes for dramatic pauses. Short punchy sentences mixed with longer ones for rhythm. Plain conversational vocabulary. Urgent-but-warm tone. Second person throughout."

**Section 5: Required Sections (Outline)**

The actual H2/H3 structure of the piece. This is the most important section of the brief - get this right and the writing becomes execution rather than invention.

For each section, specify:

- The H2 heading (final or near-final)
- What the section covers
- Approximate word count
- Required elements (statistics, examples, quotes, scriptures, etc.)
- Any subsections (H3s)

**Example outline for "What Does It Mean to Seek God's Kingdom First":**

**Introduction (150-200 words)**

- Open with the tension the reader is feeling - the squeeze of life crowding out faith
- Quote Matthew 6:33 cleanly
- Promise: by the end, you'll have both clarity and a way to actually live this
- Do not summarize the article; create momentum

**H2: What Jesus Actually Said (300 words)**

- The full passage in context (Matthew 6:25-34)
- The flow of Jesus's argument leading to verse 33
- Why context matters - this isn't a standalone command

**H2: What "Kingdom" Means Here (350 words)**

- Brief etymology without academic detour
- Kingdom as God's reign, not a place
- What seeking it actually involves (alignment, priority, allegiance)

**H2: What "First" Means in Practice (450 words)**

- First by priority, not first by sequence
- The decision-tree implication: every choice gets filtered through "does this honor God's reign?"
- Concrete examples: time, money, relationships, work

**H2: The Promise That Follows (300 words)**

- "All these things will be added"
- What this promise means and doesn't mean
- Honest treatment of tension when material things don't appear as expected

**H2: A Practical Framework for Putting This Into Daily Life (500 words)**

- 3-5 specific practices the reader can adopt this week
- Each with how-to detail, not just the principle
- Honest about what's hard

**H2: When You Fail at This (300 words)**

- Acknowledge the reality of repeatedly losing the priority
- Reframe: not perfection, but return
- Encouragement grounded in scripture

**H2: Books That Go Deeper on This \[Internal Link Section\] (200 words)**

- 2-3 free books from the library on this theme
- Brief description of each
- Direct links

**Conclusion (150 words)**

- Bring it back to the reader's original question
- Specific call to action
- Final encouragement

**Section 6: Required Elements**

**Required Statistics or Data** List specific data points that must be included, with sources.

**Required Quotes or References** Specific quotations, scriptures, expert citations.

Example: "Matthew 6:33 (NIV). Reference to Matthew 6:25-34 context. Optional: Dallas Willard on kingdom living."

**Required Examples or Stories** Specific examples that must appear. For experience-driven content, list which personal stories the author should include.

**Required Images or Media** Original photos, screenshots, charts, diagrams, embedded video. Note the alt text intent for each.

**Section 7: Internal Links**

The internal links the piece must include. Internal linking is one of the highest-leverage SEO actions on any site, and most pieces have too few.

**Required Links Out (from this piece to other pages)** Specific URLs and the anchor text for each. Aim for 5-10 internal links in any substantial piece.

Format:

- \[Anchor text\] → \[URL\] - \[why this link belongs\]

Example:

- "free book on Christian leadership" → /books/leading-like-jesus - supports cluster authority
- "what does it mean to die to self" → /blog/dying-to-self-meaning - related cluster post
- "Christian sales course rooted in service" → /books/great-selling-volume-1 - connects to commercial cluster

**Required Links In (from other pages to this piece)** Which existing pages should be updated to link to this new piece once published. This bidirectional linking accelerates ranking.

Example:

- /books/seek-first-the-kingdom (the related book) - link from book description
- /blog/dying-to-self-meaning - add as related reading
- /categories/kingdom-seeking - feature this piece as the cluster pillar

**Section 8: External Links and Citations**

**External Sources to Cite** List authoritative sources to reference, with URLs.

**External Links Policy**

- Open in new tab
- Use rel="noopener" for security
- No nofollow on legitimate citations (only for sponsored or untrusted links)
- Cite primary sources over secondary when possible

**Section 9: On-Page SEO Elements**

**Title Tag** 55-60 characters. Includes primary keyword near the start. Compelling enough to win the click against competing results.

Example: "What Does It Mean to Seek God's Kingdom First? | Team Success Network"

**Meta Description** 150-160 characters. Includes primary keyword. Promises specific value. Includes a soft call to action.

Example: "Confused by Matthew 6:33? Here's what 'seek first the kingdom' actually means in daily life - with a practical framework you can start using this week."

**URL Slug** Short, keyword-focused, no stop words.

Example: /blog/seek-gods-kingdom-first-meaning

**H1 Heading** Often the same or similar to the title tag. One H1 per page.

Example: "What Does It Mean to Seek God's Kingdom First? A Practical Guide"

**Image Alt Text Strategy** For each required image, define the alt text intent - descriptive, includes keyword where natural.

**Section 10: Schema Markup**

Specify which schema types should be applied to this piece. See references/schema-markup.md for implementation.

Common types:

- Article (almost always for blog posts)
- BlogPosting (more specific than Article)
- HowTo (for tutorial content)
- FAQPage (if the piece includes a FAQ section)
- Book (for book pages)
- Review (for review content)
- BreadcrumbList (always include if site has breadcrumbs)
- Person (for the author)

For this brief example: Article + BreadcrumbList + Person (author)

**Section 11: Conversion Path**

Every piece needs a conversion path. Without it, traffic produces nothing.

**Primary CTA** The single most important action you want the reader to take.

Example: "Sign up to receive the 7-Day Kingdom-First Reading Plan (free email series)."

**CTA Placement** Where in the piece each CTA appears. Multiple placements increase conversions; too many feel pushy. Typical structure:

- Soft mention near the introduction (no hard ask, just acknowledgment)
- Mid-content opt-in box (after the framework section)
- Closing CTA (in the conclusion)
- Sticky element (sidebar or footer bar) - site-wide, not per-piece

**Secondary CTAs** Other actions a reader might take if not ready for the primary.

Example:

- Read related books in the library
- Share the article
- Bookmark for later
- Follow on social

**Conversion Tracking** Specify what gets tracked when someone converts from this piece. The conversion event in GA4 should fire and be attributable to this URL.

**Section 12: People Also Ask (PAA) Targeting**

If the SERP shows a People Also Ask box, list the questions and decide which to address in the content.

Example PAA questions found:

- What does Matthew 6:33 mean?
- How do I seek the kingdom of God in my life?
- What is the kingdom of God?
- What does seek first the kingdom mean?

Decision: address all four within the piece. This makes the page eligible to capture multiple PAA placements as the page authority grows.

Implementation: phrase questions as H3 headings or include a dedicated FAQ section near the end with FAQPage schema.

**Section 13: Featured Snippet Targeting**

If the SERP shows a featured snippet, examine its format (paragraph, list, table) and structure your content to compete for it.

**Snippet Type Currently Showing** Paragraph / List / Table / Video / None

**Snippet Capture Strategy** A specific section of the content designed to be featured-snippet-friendly:

- For paragraph snippets: a 40-60 word direct answer near the top of the piece, immediately under an H2 phrased as a question
- For list snippets: a numbered or bulleted list under a clear question heading
- For table snippets: a clean comparison table

Example for this piece: Place a 50-word direct answer to "what does it mean to seek god's kingdom first" immediately under the H2 "What Jesus Actually Said," giving the snippet engine a clean answer to extract.

**Section 14: AI Overview Considerations**

If the SERP shows an AI Overview, note it. AI Overviews can reduce click-through rates significantly.

**AI Overview Currently Showing** Yes / No

**Strategic Response**

- Ensure the page is comprehensive enough to be cited in the AI Overview itself
- Include unique perspectives, original frameworks, or first-hand experience that AI Overviews tend to cite
- Build conversion paths that don't depend on the click - sometimes just being cited drives brand search later

**Section 15: E-E-A-T Signals**

What signals will this specific piece carry?

**Author** Named author with link to author page. For YMYL content, credentials must be visible.

**Experience Markers** Specific first-hand experience the piece will include.

Example: "Author's pastoral background and personal experience walking through career-faith tension during 2018."

**Citations** Authoritative sources cited. For Christian content, scripture citations with version, book, chapter, verse.

**Original Elements** What original perspective, framework, or content does this piece bring that isn't elsewhere?

Example: "Original 5-practice framework for daily kingdom-seeking, named and structured here for the first time."

**Section 16: Update and Maintenance Plan**

Content has a lifecycle. Plan it from the start.

**Expected Lifespan** Evergreen / Time-sensitive / Seasonal / Topical (will date quickly)

**First Review Date** When this piece should be revisited for accuracy and freshness.

Example: 12 months after publication

**Update Triggers** Events that would prompt earlier review:

- Major theological discussion shift
- New book published in the cluster
- Significant rank drop
- Major change to the related products being linked

**Section 17: Promotion Plan**

How this piece gets seen beyond passive SEO.

**Email** Will this be sent to the email list? When? With what framing?

**Social** Which platforms? What hooks per platform?

**Linking Outreach** Are there sites that would benefit from knowing this piece exists? Specific outreach plan?

**Internal Distribution** Which existing pages get updated to link to this within 7 days of publication?

**Ad Promotion** Will any paid promotion accelerate initial reach?

**Section 18: Success Metrics**

How you'll know if this piece worked.

**90-Day Metrics**

- Indexed: yes
- Earning impressions: yes (specific number realistic for the keyword)
- Earning clicks: yes (specific number)
- Average position: target range

**6-Month Metrics**

- Ranking position for primary keyword: target
- Ranking for at least 5 secondary keywords
- Conversions from the piece: target
- Internal traffic generated to linked pages: noted

**12-Month Metrics**

- Stable top-10 ranking
- Earning backlinks: at least one earned link
- Clear contribution to conversions

If a piece falls significantly short of these benchmarks at 6 months, return to the brief and identify what went wrong - usually intent mismatch, depth shortfall, or competitive misjudgment.

**Quick-Start Brief (For Simple Pieces)**

Not every piece needs the full 18-section brief. For straightforward cluster posts under 1,500 words, this minimum is sufficient:

- Primary keyword
- Search intent
- Top 3 currently ranking URLs and what's missing in them
- Word count target
- Outline (H2s with one-line descriptions)
- 5+ internal links to include with anchor text
- Title tag and meta description
- Primary CTA and placement

The full brief is for pillar pages, high-stakes commercial content, and pieces representing significant time investment. Use judgment.

**Common Mistakes To Avoid**

**Skipping SERP analysis** - writing without knowing what's currently winning is gambling. Five minutes of SERP review changes content direction substantially.

**Generic outlines** - outlines that could fit any piece on the topic produce content that ranks like any piece on the topic. Specificity in the outline produces differentiation.

**Word count cargo culting** - hitting an arbitrary word count by padding produces weaker rankings than hitting the right depth in fewer words.

**No internal links specified** - relying on the writer to "add some internal links" produces the wrong links or none. Specify them in the brief.

**No conversion path** - pieces designed to rank without converting are vanity. The CTA decision happens at brief time, not at publishing time.

**Vague tone descriptions** - "professional but friendly" produces generic content. Specific voice markers produce content that sounds like the site.

**Brief written after drafting** - the entire point is preparation. Reverse-engineering a brief from a finished draft defeats the discipline.

**Final Principle**

A great brief makes ordinary writing produce extraordinary content. A weak brief lets even great writers produce mediocre work. The brief is not paperwork - it is the strategic act of deciding what the piece is before deciding what the piece says.

Spend the time on the brief. The content production that follows will be faster, sharper, and more likely to rank. Skip the brief, and the production work doubles while results halve.

Every piece. Every time.
